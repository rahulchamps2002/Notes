Chapter 1

  1. What is Biological Anthropology? What principles does it use?
    Biological Anthropology is about studying how humans and their close relatives are different and similar in terms of bodies and genes. Principles: It follows some rules - like looking at how things have changed over time, considering everything about humans, understanding how they adapt to survive, and studying how traits are passed from parents to kids.
  2. What are the subfields of Anthropology?
    Anthropology has different parts. We have cultural anthropology (looking at how people live), archaeology (digging up old stuff), linguistic anthropology (studying how language is used), and biological anthropology.
  3. Name the different related disciplines under Biological Anthropology and explain what they study.
    In Biological Anthropology, we have parts like primatology (studying monkeys and apes), human evolutionary biology (learning about how humans changed over a long time), paleoanthropology (looking at old human fossils), and human biology (seeing how different groups of people today are different biologically).
  4. What is the scientific method? Explain the components of the scientific method. What is an empirical process?
    The scientific method is like a step-by-step way scientists figure out answers to questions or problems. Components: First, you look at something interesting. Then, you ask a clear question about it. After that, you make a guess (hypothesis) about the answer. Next, you do tests to check if your guess is right. You write down what you see and measure during the tests (data collection). Then, you look at your notes to figure out what they mean (analysis). Finally, you sum up what you found out (conclusion). Empirical Process: It means using evidence from watching, measuring, or testing things directly instead of just thinking about it. It's like saying, "I know because I saw it happen!"

    Darwin was based on concept of natural selection while Lamarck is based on the concept of internal vital force which is present in all living organisms. Lamarck believe organisms developed traits during their life. Darwin believed organisms were born with it.

Chapter 3

  1. What is the difference between prokaryotes and eukaryotes?
    • Prokaryotes: Unicellular organisms that lack a true nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles.
    • Eukaryotes: Unicellular and Multicellular organisms that have a true nucleus and various membrane-bound organelles.
  2. Describe the types of cells in humans. What is the function of each? Where do we find them?
    • Stem Cells: Found in the bone marrow, umbilical cord blood, and certain tissues. Repair tissues, regenerate organs and disease treatment.
    • Bone Cells: Found in the bones of a person. Maintain and remodel bone tissue while marginalizing calcim balance.
    • Blood Cells: Found in the blood streams. Vital for oxygen transport, immune defense, and clotting.
    • Muscle Cells: Found in the muscles. Enable movements such as contraction and extension.
    • Nerve Cells: Found in the brain and nervous system. Transmit electrical signals for communication.
    • Epithelial Cells: Airways, outerlayer of skin, and the gut. Line surfaces, protect, and absorb.
    • Liver Cells: Found in the liver. Detoxify, metabolize, and produce bile.
    • Fat Cells: Found in adipose tissue around the body. Store energy as fat.
    • Immune Cells: Found all across the body. Defend against pathogens.
    • Reproductive Cells: Found in testis or ovaries. Responsible for reproduction.
  3. How many chromosomes does each cell type have?
    • Somatic Cells: 46 chromosomes
    • Gamete Cells: 23 chromosomes
  4. What are DNA and RNA? Where are they located? What is their difference?
    • DNA: Deoxyribonucleic Acid: long-term storage of genetic information. Double helixed which contain phosphate, 5-carbon sugar, and nitrogenous bases (Adenine, Thymine, Guanin, and Cytosine).
    • RNA: Ribonucleic Acid: messenger between DNA and protein synthesis. Single stranded and shorter than DNA.
  5. Describe the composition of DNA.
    DNA consists of Phosphate, 5-carbon sugar, and nitrogenous bases Adenine, Thymine, Guanine, and Cytosine. Adenine pairs with Thymine and Guanine pairs with Cytosine.
  6. What forms a nucleotide?
    Nucleotides are the essential building blocks of DNA and RNA. They are made from the information above. In RNA, Uracil (U) replaces Thymine.
  7. Why are triplets of nucleotides important?
    Triplets start and stop codons and help allowing protein synthesis.
  8. How does DNA replicate?
    DNA is replicated through Protein Synthesis.
  9. Describe what proteins are and their function.
    Proteins are complex molecules that help structure and support, catalyze chimical reactions with enzymes, transport and storage, immune defense, cell signaling, and providing energy sources.
  10. Describe the process of protein synthesis. Where do the different processes occur?
    Protein Synthesis consists of transcription and translation. Transcription occurs in the nucleus of the cell where RNA polymerases converts DNA sequences to mRNA. Translation occurs in the ribosomes.
  11. What happens during transcription, translation?
    Transcription converts DNA sequences to mRNA. Translation is the process where the mRNA is decoded to produce a polypeptide chain (protein).
  12. Describe the different kinds of cell division.
    • Mitosis: A process of cell duplication durting which one cell gives rise to two genetically identical daughter cells.
    • Meiosis: A type of cell division that produces four haploid daughter cells from a single cell.
